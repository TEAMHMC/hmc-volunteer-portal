The Program

A curriculum. A movement. A platform.

Unstoppable began as a five-module healing-centered workshop addressing mental health in Black communities, the intersectionality of race and disability, and systemic barriers to care. The participant data it generated, published by the Los Angeles County Department of Mental Health, shaped every digital tool HMC has since built.

Event Formats

Three Ways to Experience Unstoppable.

Whether you want to move your body, sit in conversation, or connect virtually, there is a format that meets you where you are.

Format 01

MOVE

Live Walk & Run

Community movement for mental health. All fitness levels welcome. Walk, run, or roll alongside your neighbors while engaging in facilitated conversations about healing and well-being.

Free

Format 02

HEAL

Wellness Meetup

Healing conversation space. Connect, share, and build in a culturally affirming environment. Facilitated by community health workers trained in Unstoppable curriculum.

Free

Interactive virtual format. Live discussions, expert guests, and community connection. Participate from anywhere in LA County with an internet connection.

Key Findings

The Report.

Health Matters Clinic measured the impact of the Unstoppable workshop using a retrospective pre-then-post evaluation, presented to the Los Angeles County Department of Mental Health. The results were clear: healing-centered, culturally affirming mental health education works.

Case Study 01

"Access isn't the finish line. Real change means breaking stigma, rebuilding trust, and honoring lived experience."

Even with more people talking about mental health, many Black folks still feel scared to reach out, after too many bad experiences with the system.

Alang, S. (2019). Addressing mental health stigma in African American communities. Journal of Health Care for the Poor and Underserved, 30(1), 4 to 10.

Read the case study
Case Study 02

"Moving isn't just about a new address. It can shake who you are."

For Black teens, migrating during adolescence can double the risk of psychosis. Care has to be culturally rooted, trauma aware, and built for the journey.

Ayon, C., and Garcia, S. J. (2023). The impact of migration on mental health in Black adolescents. PLOS Mental Health.

Read the case study
Case Study 03

"Three walls block the way: stigma, no trust, and no way to get there."

To reach Black youth, we have to meet them where they are, with care that is real, not just clinical, in schools, homes, and community spaces that feel safe.

Lindsey, M. A., Joe, S., and Nebbitt, V. (2010). Barriers to mental health services for African American youth. Children and Youth Services Review, 32(6), 760 to 768.

Read the case study
Case Study 04

"Pushing through isn't always power. Sometimes it's pain in disguise."

Always holding it together, without rest, without room to fall apart, can lead to anxiety, depression, and burnout. Vulnerability is survival evolved.

Abrams, J. A., Hill, A., and Maxwell, M. (2024). The strong Black woman schema and its impact on mental health. Journal of Black Psychology.

Read the case study
Case Study 05

"Feeling is not weakness. It's power."

Black men carry invisible burdens. Pressures to man up, stay silent, and push through are costing lives. Change starts by flipping the script on masculinity.

Williams, D. R. (2021). Addressing mental health disparities in Black men. APA Monitor on Psychology, 52(7), 24 to 31.
