Street Medicine Outreach — What you need to know about our screening protocols and when to escalate
Who is this for? All volunteers at HMC street medicine events. You do not need to be a licensed medical professional to read and understand this guide. Clinical decisions are always made by the on-site Supervising Practitioner (PA) — your job is to know the basics so you can assist the team and escalate when needed.

The Traffic-Light Escalation System

HMC uses a simple three-color system at every event to quickly communicate how urgent a participant's situation is. Every volunteer must understand this system.

GREEN — Routine

  • Participant is stable with no urgent concerns
  • Screening results are within normal ranges
  • Continue with standard screening flow and health education
  • Offer non-urgent follow-up referrals as appropriate (e.g., "See your doctor in 3–6 months")

Your action: Proceed normally. No escalation needed.

YELLOW — Urgent (Abnormal Finding)

  • Screening results are outside normal range but not immediately life-threatening
  • Examples: high blood pressure reading, elevated blood sugar, reactive HIV test
  • The PA must review the results same-day
  • Participant gets a priority referral with specific next steps
  • Follow-up expected within days to weeks

Your action: Notify the PA or lead clinician immediately. Stay calm, reassure the participant: "Your results show a finding that may need follow-up. This doesn't mean you definitely have a condition — it's important to see a doctor for a full check." Help connect them with a referral.

RED — Emergency (Life-Threatening)

  • Participant shows signs of a life-threatening situation
  • Examples: extremely high blood pressure (180/120+), dangerously low blood sugar, loss of consciousness, chest pain, difficulty breathing, seizure

Your action: Immediately notify the PA. The PA will assess and decide if 911 is needed. Stay with the participant — do not leave them alone. Keep them calm. Clear the area if needed.

When to call 911 (anyone can do this): Chest pain or pressure • Difficulty breathing • Stroke symptoms (Face drooping, Arm weakness, Speech difficulty — remember FAST) • Loss of consciousness • Seizure • Severe allergic reaction • Active suicidal ideation with intent • Any situation where life appears in immediate danger. Call 911 first, then notify the PA.

Screening Protocols at a Glance

Below are the clinical screenings HMC performs at street medicine events. You may not perform all of these yourself — your role assignment determines which station you work at. This overview helps you understand what's happening around you and when something needs to be escalated.

Blood Pressure Screening

Measured with a blood pressure cuff after the participant has been seated and rested for at least 5 minutes.

A small finger-prick test using a glucometer. Results depend on whether the participant has eaten recently.

HIV Rapid Testing (OraQuick)

An oral swab self-test. Participant must not have eaten or drunk anything in the last 20 minutes. Results are read after 20 minutes.

  • Non-reactive (negative): Counsel on prevention and PrEP availability — GREEN
  • Reactive (preliminary positive): Provide supportive, non-judgmental counseling. Refer for confirmatory lab testing and linkage to care. Provide resource card — YELLOW
  • Invalid: Re-test with a new kit. If second test is also invalid, refer for lab testing
A reactive result is NOT a diagnosis. It means further testing is needed. Communicate this clearly and compassionately.

Naloxone (Narcan) Distribution

Naloxone is a nasal spray that can reverse an opioid overdose. California law allows distribution without a prescription.

  • Provide brief overdose recognition education: person is unresponsive, breathing is slow or stopped, lips/fingertips are blue
  • Demonstrate how to use the nasal spray
  • Distribute the kit and log on the Naloxone Distribution Log

Emergency Response

If you encounter a life-threatening emergency:

  1. Make sure the scene is safe for you and the participant
  2. Call 911
  3. Notify the PA / lead clinician immediately
  4. Stay with the participant — do not leave them alone
  5. If trained: begin CPR/first aid as appropriate
  6. If opioid overdose suspected: administer naloxone
  7. Clear the area and have someone meet the ambulance

Key Reminders

  • You are not expected to diagnose anyone. HMC provides screenings, not diagnoses. If anything looks abnormal, escalate to the PA.
  • When in doubt, escalate. It is always better to flag something that turns out to be fine than to miss something important.
  • Use the buddy system — never separate from your partner.
  • No PHI on personal devices — no photos, no notes with participant names on your phone.
  • Stay in your lane. Non-clinical volunteers do not give medical advice. If someone asks a medical question, bring them to a clinical team member.
